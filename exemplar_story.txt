At 06:12, twelve hours after Elena Rossi vanished, Detective Lydia Reed was called in to Harborview PD. Elena’s supervisor from Planning kept repeating the same few facts as if saying them more cleanly might change them: Elena had worked late, sent an email with updated waterfront documents, and then gone dark. IT confirmed the email. Her phone’s last ping put her in Harborview’s old shipyard district. Lydia listened, already moving, because the gap between “last seen” and “last found” had teeth, and because someone was already trying to turn time into a weapon. By the time she reached the Dockside Shipyard Annex, patrol tape was up around Bay C, but it had the sloppy look of a perimeter thrown fast and left to hold itself. Lydia found what they missed: a narrow service corridor that led to a mezzanine, the kind of overlooked route that made an abduction possible without a crowd ever noticing.

On the interior stairs she saw Elena’s red scarf snagged on a rusted bolt, the fabric pulled tight as if it had tried to resist. On the floor below was a fresh muddy boot print—industrial tread, size 11—tracking toward the stairwell. In a floor grate she found a USB drive, and when she accessed it she saw Elena’s revised plan beside a second copy that had been altered and redacted. Nearby lay a torn piece of blueprint with Gate 3 circled and a smudged warning that read, “Meet after hours—trust no one.” Near a doorway she spotted a mangled security badge, its mag-stripe scraped clean. Above the entrance, a security camera caught only a hooded silhouette entering, not stumbling like a trespasser but moving with care, as if the camera’s existence was part of the plan. Lydia called for forensics, but she didn’t wait for them to tell her what she could already feel: this wasn’t a disappearance that happened in the dark by accident. She looked at the time, did the arithmetic she hated doing, and started the clock in her head—twelve hours left.

She had Bay C re-walked with a tighter grid, because the first pass through a scene was where people found what they expected, not what was real. In the corridor patrol had missed, Lydia found a second muddy tread mark with the same industrial pattern and the same size, but it didn’t go toward the stairs. It angled to a side access panel. When they opened it, it led to an exterior catwalk over the water—an exit route that avoided the main roll-up door and the eyes that might have been there. Wedged behind the hinge, tucked out of sight with intent, were two items that didn’t belong in a shipyard annex: a glossy city-sponsored fundraiser wristband and a folded valet claim ticket stamped 11:07 p.m. The fundraiser venue matched the one Jonathan Reed had said he attended. The wristband’s serial number came back as legitimately issued, but not to Jonathan. It was logged under a vague entry: “J. Reed +1,” the kind of add-on that could slip by when crowds were thick and staff were tired.

Before Lydia could finish photographing the hinge area, a radio call pulled her away: the USB drive’s evidence bag at the temporary table had a compromised seal. The officer reporting it offered “could be accidental” in the same tone people used when they wanted their words to become permission to stop looking. Lydia didn’t give it. She locked down evidence handling and started a quiet audit of who’d had physical access to the USB since she’d recovered it. The wristband and the valet ticket were already doing damage, because they didn’t just suggest a connection—they suggested a story that would run ahead of her, and she could hear it forming: Reed, scandal, motive. She ordered fundraiser security footage pulled immediately, not because she believed it would clear Jonathan, but because she needed to know who was wearing that “+1” like a mask.

The next shove came from inside her own building. Internal Affairs arrived at Bay C—not because Elena was missing, but because someone had filed an evidence-handling complaint about the compromised USB seal minutes earlier. IA insisted the drive be transferred to central evidence “for integrity,” which meant prying the one item that directly tied to Elena’s work out of Lydia’s hands. Lydia refused to sign any transfer without an on-site re-seal and documented handoff. The argument ate minutes that she could feel falling away, each one a unit of air Elena might not have. While IA recited procedure, Lydia did the only thing she could: she had a tech quietly pull the fundraiser’s wristband scan history and valet system snapshots before anyone could sanitize them. The check-in showed “J. Reed +1” entered at 7:42 p.m., but the +1 field had no ID and no signature. The valet ticket #A-1887 stamped 11:07 p.m. had been redeemed by a woman in a dark hooded coat, filmed from an angle too low to catch her face—only her hands. And those hands were in a thin nitrile glove, strange at a fundraiser, unmistakable as a choice. Jonathan became the media’s focal suspect overnight because the evidence was shaped to make him one, and Lydia marked the shape of it: strong enough to drag him into the spotlight, too clean to trust.

Because the torn blueprint had circled Gate 3, Lydia used what authority she could still exercise under IA’s shadow and pushed for the Dockside Shipyard access history. What she was given wasn’t a digital record but a manual after-hours logbook, sloppy and smudged and out of order—exactly the kind of document that could be “managed” without leaving a clean trace. Two entries mattered. One read “City Planning — urgent packet drop” at 9:18 p.m., signed with an illegible scrawl that, at first glance, looked like “P. Nair.” The second read “Contractor—equipment check” at 12:26 a.m., with a badge number only partially written, trailing off into “…72.” Lydia noticed the 9:18 entry was inked with a different pen type than the lines above and below it, and the paper beneath showed fresh indentation as if it had been written later on top of older pages. Before she could photograph the full page, an IA lieutenant ordered the area cleared for “evidence integrity,” and shipyard security instructed the foreman to pocket the logbook. Lydia managed one partial photo as it disappeared—just the lower corner: 12:26 a.m., “…72,” and a grease smear shaped like a thumbprint. It wasn’t enough to prove what she’d seen, but it was enough to tell her someone didn’t want it documented.

Minutes later her rapid-ID unit told her the valet redeemer footage was “temporarily unavailable due to a system update.” The timing was too precise to be coincidence. Lydia held her ground with IA, refusing to sign over the USB until she got a full photograph of the Gate 3 page. IA threatened to seize the drive as “city property.” The foreman vanished into a back room with the logbook and returned with a different one, calling it a duplicate copy; it conveniently didn’t include the 12:26 a.m. entry at all, and the 9:18 “City Planning—urgent packet drop” looked rewritten in a neater hand. Lydia stepped outside to call her unit lead, and her phone buzzed with an automated alert: her partial logbook photo had been remotely flagged and removed from the shared case drive under an “integrity hold.” It wasn’t just obstruction anymore. It was erasure. Lydia sent the image directly to an off-network contact in records and ordered her team to pull vendor rosters for the contractor badge batch ending in 72 before those records could be “updated” into a lie.

The badge-format check came back: “…72” matched a Harborview contractors’ credential batch issued through a third-party access vendor used on the waterfront project. Two names in that batch jumped out—Diego Alvarez, expected because of his contractor ties, and a subcontracted data-cabling and comms vendor that did overnight work in city facilities, a bridge between “City Planning” and “Contractor” that felt deliberately convenient. When the roster dump arrived, it came as a PDF instead of a native export, and the metadata showed it had been generated only fourteen minutes earlier, not pulled from archives. It listed three badge IDs ending in “…72” active the night Elena disappeared: one assigned to a waterfront comms subcontractor runner with a generic name, “J. Morales,” one unassigned, and one assigned to Diego. But the badge photo attached to “Diego Alvarez” didn’t match the Diego in Lydia’s file. Different jawline, different ear shape. It was a template swap—someone building a version of Diego who fit the Gate 3 story. When Lydia called the vendor’s after-hours support line, the rep insisted the PDF was “standard,” then slipped and admitted an “IA liaison” had requested the roster be “cleaned up for integrity” before release.

Lydia drove back to Gate 3 to test paper against metal. The foreman now claimed the card reader had been offline for maintenance that night, contradicting what she’d been told earlier. Lydia spotted fresh tool marks on the reader panel as if someone had opened it recently. When she tried to photograph the tamper screws and serial label, shipyard security moved in with a speed that didn’t match routine enforcement. They cited trespass and “critical infrastructure,” blocked her camera, and tried to take her phone for “chain of custody,” repeating the same play from City Hall and Bay C. Lydia refused, backed out, and airdropped the photos to her off-network records contact from the parking lot. An anonymous tip hit dispatch almost immediately—Diego Alvarez had been seen near Shipyard Annex Bay C around midnight with a woman—perfectly timed to match the doctored roster and the missing 12:26 entry. Lydia logged it as a constructed narrative. It still forced time and manpower to bleed away, which was the point. In her car, she noticed a faint footer line on the vendor PDF: an internal tracking code indicating the file had passed through an edit station at City Hall, not the vendor’s system. Someone wasn’t just cleaning records. They were laundering them inside the city.

She went off-network and used a favor from an IT contact in Records to locate the edit station. It resolved to a locked multi-use workstation cluster on City Hall’s fourth floor shared by Procurement and Internal Affairs liaisons after hours—the kind of bureaucratic choke point where reality could be turned into a PDF and stamped as “official.” The door was already ajar when she arrived. The room looked staged to appear harmless: lights on, a maintenance cone, a wiped keyboard. Lydia began photographing serial tags, cable routing, and a printed sign-in sheet, but a security supervisor she didn’t recognize stepped in with an emergency directive: IA had restricted access to “systems involved in an active integrity review,” and he demanded Lydia surrender her phone for chain-of-custody. Lydia refused and forced a compromise: she would leave the hallway, but she would keep her phone. As she backed out, she spotted a slip of paper half-shoved under a monitor stand, left in haste rather than planted with care. It was a Procurement routing cover sheet with three items circled: “Harborview Access Solutions — badge services renewal (expedite),” “Shipyard perimeter reader replacement — Gate 3 (rush),” and “Data-model support contract — Planning Dept (junior analyst assignment).” The last line carried a name—Mei Lin Chen—attached to a “temporary vendor liaison” role Lydia had never seen in Mei Lin’s HR file. Lydia snapped a partial photo, and caught one more detail on the sign-in sheet before the room was sealed: a handwritten visitor name from earlier that night, “J. Morales,” the same runner identity from the compromised roster.

With the clock on her mind dropping under ten hours, Lydia left City Hall and drove toward the shipyard district again, not to chase Diego but to press the Procurement circles against something physical. At the perimeter fence, Gate 3 now wore a brand-new reader housing, still smelling of fresh sealant, mounted slightly crooked as if rushed. Inside the guard kiosk, zip-tied where it could be found and cited as “temporary,” was an access log printout labeled “TEMP—FOR IA,” showing Gate 3 “maintenance bypass” active for a twenty-minute window the night Elena disappeared. The guard refused to let Lydia photograph it, insisting IA had ordered everyone to treat Gate 3 as sensitive infrastructure. While Lydia argued, an unknown number texted her: stop digging into Procurement; you’re late already; check Bay C again—she’s still there. Lydia heard the tactic: engineered urgency aimed at the wrong location. Instead of Bay C, she walked the perimeter line and found fresh truck ruts in the mud leading away from Gate 3 toward an unmarked service road behind the annex. Beside the ruts lay a broken plastic clip used on visitor badges, and a torn corner of paper with a partial vendor code: “H.A.S—.” Before she could bag it, a maintenance cart rolled up and two workers in reflective vests—too clean for the yard—claimed an IA work order and tried to herd her away, positioning themselves between Lydia and the tracks. As she backed off, she saw size-11 industrial tread prints overlaid on the ruts, fresh and deliberate, moving from Gate 3 toward the service road.

She followed the ruts onto the unmarked service road and found a recessed turnout screened by scrub and chain-link, dressed to look abandoned with scattered pallets, an empty drum, and a torn tarp weighted with bricks. Under the tarp sat a city-issued orange lockbox used for temporary field keys, forced open but not emptied. Inside was a carbon-copy Vehicle Movement Log sheet stamped “HARBORVIEW IA—TEMP” with a single entry: “Unit: H.A.S. Service / Time: 22:41–23:03 / Purpose: Reader swap—Gate 3.” The driver line was smeared, but the initials block showed “M.L.C.” faintly impressed through the paper. There was also a printed route slip for a municipal cargo van directing it to “Annex perimeter—Gate 3” and then “Return via Service Rd K.” Caught in the lockbox hinge was a thin strip of Elena’s scarf fabric with the same weave and dye as the piece recovered at the annex stairs. Before Lydia could photograph everything, one of the “maintenance” men stepped into the turnout with a phone already raised, loudly narrating that Detective Reed couldn’t be there because it was IA-controlled infrastructure, and reached for the lockbox as if it belonged to him. Lydia snapped one quick photo—enough to capture H.A.S., the time window, and the faint M.L.C. impression—and pocketed the scarf strip. As she pulled away, a burner email hit her phone with the subject line “PRIORITY—DIEGO ALVAREZ STORAGE KEY” and a photo of a key tag labeled “BAY C—LOCKER 12.” It was staged to feel like a breakthrough and timed to pull her back to Bay C.

She refused to abandon Gate 3 entirely, but she couldn’t ignore the bait without checking that it wasn’t a trap that hid something real. Lydia delegated a uniformed unit to keep eyes on the service road turnout while she and her partner cut back to the Dockside Shipyard Annex to check Bay C, Locker 12—fast, controlled, in and out. Inside Bay C, Locker 12 sat with its padlock already missing, as if someone wanted the door opened and the contents seen. The key tag in the photo hung from the latch. Inside was a contractor tool roll stamped with a subcontractor name tied to Diego Alvarez’s work history, a single disposable glove with diesel residue on the fingertips, a municipal route strip that looked relevant until Lydia saw the faint gray header reading “TRAINING / SAMPLE,” and a cheap prepaid phone box, empty, with a smudged label reading “H.A.S. Service.” It was a clean bridge between Diego, H.A.S., and Gate 3, too clean to be anything but manufactured. Lydia began to photograph the glove, and then the building itself shifted against her: a forklift engine coughed to life deeper in the annex, rolled just enough to block the main bay exit, and forced her back through the narrow service corridor. The overhead sodium light flickered and died, dropping Bay C into near-darkness. It wasn’t a confrontation, it was containment—delay and isolation without a face to arrest. When the emergency light came on and Lydia got out, the forklift sat positioned to look like ordinary equipment left by workers, and any footprints around Locker 12 were smeared by fresh tire tracks. Lydia pocketed the “TRAINING/SAMPLE” printout and texted her unit at the turnout: do not lose that spot; if anyone shows, detain and ID.

Back at Service Road K, with her clock sliding further down into the eight-hour range, she got her first clean pressure test. A white municipal utility van rolled in slow with headlights off until the last second, as if the driver expected the turnout to be empty. Her unit moved to detain. The driver wasn’t Diego. He was a contracted courier with a city work-order clipboard and a sealed envelope marked “HARBORVIEW PLANNING — ATTN: E. ROSSI.” He said he’d been paid cash to drop it at the turnout and photograph it on the guardrail as proof of delivery. There was no sender name and no return address, only a printed job code: “H.A.S. / 03-GATE.” Inside the envelope was a printed screenshot of Elena’s own email—timestamped, as if someone wanted Lydia to believe the documents had been intercepted and redistributed—along with a prepaid phone SIM taped to the paper bearing four words: “ASK PRIYA ABOUT TIF.” There was also a strip of Elena’s red scarf fabric, too clean to have come from the shipyard snag, suggesting it had been cut later and planted. The message was specific enough to drag Lydia toward Priya Nair and budget motives, and it was also too on-the-nose, matching the pattern: cohesive, curated, and meant to steer her away from the Gate 3 and IA route.

As Lydia ordered the envelope and SIM bagged, the courier’s van suddenly locked and began auto-lock cycling. The courier swore he hadn’t touched anything. At the same moment Lydia’s phone briefly lost service, then returned with a single incoming text from an unknown number: “7:30. Bring the SAMPLE paper. Come alone.” The sender knew what she’d taken from Locker 12, which meant they were tracking her movements or listening through channels she couldn’t trust. She kept the perimeter unit on the turnout and backtracked the courier’s phone. There were no calls, only a camera app, and a single recent GPS pin saved as “03-GATE.” Under pressure, the courier admitted he’d never met the employer. Instructions came by text, payments were cash, and the pickup spot changed nightly. Tonight’s instruction included one new line: “If she brings the SAMPLE, lock her in.”

Lydia didn’t go alone. She sent a plainclothes team to shadow at a distance and put a tech in an unmarked car with a spectrum scanner. At 7:30 she arrived at the designated pull-off carrying the “TRAINING/SAMPLE” route sheet in an evidence bag. A message hit immediately: prove you’re not recording; put your phone on the guardrail. When she set it down, her signal bars dropped to nothing. The scanner spiked—localized interference, tight radius, deliberate. A municipal gate arm she hadn’t noticed, collapsed in weeds like something forgotten, whirred to life and swung shut across the turnout as if remotely triggered. Nearby, the utility van’s locks cycled again, louder, like an acknowledgment that the trap had taken hold. Lydia stepped away from the guardrail and watched the tree line. Headlights bloomed as another vehicle approached, moving slow with no beams until the last second. It didn’t stop. It threw a small hard case out the passenger window and accelerated away.

Inside the case was a printed memo on Harborview City letterhead: “Suspend Rossi revisions pending TIF compliance review — Priya Nair.” It was clean and crisp, formatted like an internal document, except the footer used an outdated department seal. Lydia recognized it for what it was: a forgery meant to do real damage even if it was false. The case also held a burner phone already powered on with a draft message addressed to Lydia—“Stop looking at Gate 3”—and a single industrial work glove, size large, with dried mud on the cuff. Before Lydia could bag everything, the burner phone lit up with an incoming call labeled only “H.A.S.” When she answered, a voice modulator delivered one sentence: she was late to the truth; check Jonathan’s fundraiser photos and look at who was behind him. Then the line died. The interference released at the same instant, as if the caller wanted the call to land but not be traced.

Lydia pulled the fundraiser footage on-site. In the background, half-hidden behind Jonathan Reed, she spotted a familiar face from Planning: Mei Lin Chen. On Mei Lin’s wrist was a distinctive band that matched what Lydia had seen earlier on the hooded silhouette in the Dockside Shipyard Annex camera clip. Lydia pulled off the road and handed the hard case, the burner, and the glove to her tech for processing—fiber, mud composition, any accelerant or diesel residue that could match what they’d seen at the annex. She called her captain and ordered quiet containment on Mei Lin—no approach, no call, no badge-swipe alert—because she needed Mei Lin to keep moving while Lydia built the lock around her. The adversary fired back immediately. Streetlights along the shipyard access road blinked out in sequence like breadcrumbs dragging responders toward Gate 3. Dispatch logs filled with false alarms within three blocks—fire panel trips, then a “person down” call—splitting patrol coverage. Lydia’s unit lost GPS lock twice in brief micro-jams that didn’t black out communications completely, just enough to desynchronize navigation and timestamps. Lydia recognized it as traffic-shaping: not hiding from the investigation, but steering it.

A lab tech reported that the dried mud on the glove contained crushed shell aggregate used at Harborview’s private marina renovation site, a project publicly tied to Diego Alvarez’s contractor circle, a clean arrow toward Diego that fit too neatly with everything else being staged. Lydia didn’t take the bait; she logged it and assigned a detective to verify whether the aggregate was truly unique or merely marketed as such. Then Internal Affairs hit her with a new obstruction: a city network audit flagged that Lydia’s credentials had been used to query municipal gate-arm controls minutes before the gate swung shut on her. The audit was designed to isolate her and force her into panic, but Lydia saw the tell. The access log showed a login method her account could not perform—a legacy token protocol decommissioned the previous year. Someone was spoofing her identity to create friction and slow the rescue.

She went back to the one place where the adversary couldn’t rewrite reality as easily: timestamps and images. She pulled the full-resolution fundraiser gallery again and zoomed past Jonathan’s shoulder. Mei Lin’s wristband was visible, and so was something else: a lanyard clip marked with a Harborview Asset Systems vendor insignia—the same initials as H.A.S. from the documents, the lockbox log, the courier code, the call label. It wasn’t a tag meant for a party. It was access. Lydia ordered a search warrant request for Mei Lin Chen’s desk, locker, and home for any H.A.S.-issued tokens, key fobs, or vendor credentials, and a quiet check of Harborview Asset Systems’ overnight maintenance roster for who had remote privileges for municipal gate arms and shipyard lighting controls. Before she could finish pushing the request through, the burner rang again—H.A.S.—and the modulated voice delivered a final threat: last hour; if she went to Mei Lin, Elena would die. Lydia ended the call without replying and forwarded the audio for extraction. Then she made the decision the kidnapper didn’t want her to make. She stopped chasing Gate 3 entirely and pivoted to what the interference implied: a detention site tied into the same municipal network—somewhere remote control of lights, gate arms, and alarm routing mattered, like a city-controlled utility substation or pump facility.

With less than an hour left on her mental countdown, an auditor from Planning called with a rushed update: Priya Nair’s cost-center had authorized an after-hours “emergency maintenance” purchase order payable to a subcontractor sharing a mailing address with a shell tied to the redevelopment consortium. It was incriminating enough to justify pulling Lydia off course, and it arrived fast enough to feel like another script being handed to her. Lydia ordered the trail preserved and refused to divert. As her convoy turned toward the pump facility access road, the city grid flickered in a rolling wave—streetlights strobing, dying block by block. Dispatch received a “gas leak” auto-alarm at the facility and another two blocks away, pushing fire to stage and threatening to lock Lydia out behind hazmat protocol. Her MDT showed a fresh municipal lockdown command on the facility gates entered under her name. Lydia used the same fabrication tell—legacy token method—and had her tech bypass the gate locally while she kept communications on the clean line and ordered patrol to hold perimeter rather than chase false calls.

Inside the service yard she spotted a glossy new H.A.S. vendor lockout tag placed on a control cabinet like a signpost, meant to be noticed. She ignored it and followed what couldn’t be faked quickly: sound and airflow. A low generator hum came from a sealed maintenance corridor that shouldn’t have been active. In the corridor Lydia found Elena Rossi bound but alive in a maintenance alcove beside a live control rack, placed close enough to make forced entry risky. Elena’s scarf was missing its torn end; the snagged piece at Dockside confirmed the struggle point had been the annex, not this holding site. Mei Lin Chen stepped out from behind the rack wearing a Harborview Asset Systems vendor jacket, holding a phone wired to a relay module. She had been using vendor privileges to manipulate lights, gate arms, and alarm routing, not as an escape plan but as a method to herd responders and buy time.

Mei Lin’s demand was simple and narrow: Elena had to retract the discrepancy report, and Lydia had to stop digging into the redevelopment plan’s data model. She referenced the altered files on the USB, proof she had already tried to rewrite the record. Lydia kept her talking, refusing sudden motion and refusing to give Mei Lin the moment she wanted. In the details Mei Lin gave to threaten and control, she explained one thing too many about the network. Lydia’s tech used that information to isolate the rack and kill the relay without dropping power to the room. When the relay went dead, Mei Lin lost the ability to trigger a harmful surge or lock doors. Lydia moved in, arrested her, and got Elena out through the service yard to a waiting medic unit.

After, the red herrings that had been thrown like flares resolved into what they had always been: Jonathan Reed’s fundraiser window remained solid and time-stamped—his motive real, his presence not; the shell aggregate in the glove mud proved widely distributed fill used across multiple waterfront jobs, easy to plant and not unique; the “emergency maintenance” PO under Priya’s cost-center traced back to an automated rollover template that Mei Lin could access through the data team’s cross-department permissions, a paper trail designed to point away from her. Elena, shaken but coherent, confirmed the “Gate 3 after hours” note had been a meeting request routed through internal channels—something only a colleague with access could convincingly stage. Lydia watched the facility lights stabilize and, as Elena’s stretcher cleared the ambulance threshold, felt the last seconds of her countdown fall away into zero—not as a victory lap, but as a hard confirmation that someone had tried to turn the city itself into a set of locks, and had almost succeeded.
